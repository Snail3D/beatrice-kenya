A few words for you to hear first

Beatrice is opening a window restaurant from her little house in Western Kenya. It is not a fancy place. It is a window, a counter, a stove, a kettle, and a mother who knows how to feed people.

Two big crowds walk past her gate every single day: the college students who need a hot plate before class, and the hospital workers who need a fast meal on a long shift. They will come back when the food is good and the price is fair. That is the whole business.

"Chakula maber, pile maber, thuolo maber. Ka iyie winjo, iyawo biro."
Good food, good welcome, good measure. When you hear about it, they will come.

The plan is simple: cook five or six things very well. Set up an M-Pesa till. Open WhatsApp. Hand out a few free plates to the nurses. Tell the students to spread the word. Within one month, the window will be known.

How to Pay

Yot unyalo chulo

M-Pesa Till Number: to be printed on the window once set up
Cash: always welcome, always have small change ready
WhatsApp Order: send a message, pay on pickup, walk away happy

Tip: the till number is for customers, not for you. It is their number to send to, not for you to receive. That small detail is what makes the difference between a professional and a hobby.

1

Food Cost Percentage

Every dish should cost you no more than 35 percent of what you sell it for. If your plate costs more than that to make, you are working for the food, not the food working for you.

food cost % = (ingredients cost / selling price) × 100
Example: Ugali and fish. The fish (200 g) is KSh 120. The ugali flour (100 g) is KSh 12. The oil and salt is KSh 15. Total ingredients: KSh 147.
You sell the plate for KSh 250. So: (147 / 250) × 100 = 58.8 percent.
This plate is too expensive to make. Either raise the price to KSh 450, or buy smaller fish, or reduce the portion. Do not just accept it.

The healthier number for a window restaurant is 25 to 35 percent. Anything above 40 means your profit is too thin and one bad day will wipe out a week of work.

2

Daily Break-Even

How many plates do you need to sell each day before you start making a profit, not just covering costs. This is the number that tells you when to keep cooking and when to close early.

break-even plates = daily fixed costs / average profit per plate
Example: Your fixed costs each day (rent share, charcoal, kerosene, water, phone, M-Pesa fees) = KSh 600. Your average profit per plate (selling price minus ingredients minus gas) = KSh 120.
Break-even: 600 / 120 = 5 plates per day just to cover your costs.
Every plate after plate number 5 is money in your pocket. Plate number 25 is real profit. Plate number 40 is a good day.

For a Western Kenya window restaurant, 25 to 40 plates per day is a healthy working week. Less than 15 means you are losing money and need to adjust prices or find more customers.

3

Reorder Point

Never run out of fish on a Friday. Never run out of ugali flour on a Saturday morning. This formula tells you when to walk to the market.

reorder point = (average daily usage × lead time in days) + safety stock
Example: You use about 2 kg of fish per day. The market is 2 days away (today and Saturday, market day is Monday). You want a 1-day safety buffer so you do not run dry.
Reorder point: (2 × 2) + 2 = 6 kg remaining.
When your fish gets down to 6 kg, send someone to the market the next morning. REORDER AT 6 KG

Lead time means how many days between realizing you need something and having it in your kitchen. In rural Kenya, that is usually 1 to 3 days depending on the item and your transport.

4

Profit Split

A simple rule that keeps the business alive and the household fed at the same time. Do not skip this. Every shilling that comes in has a job before it gets spent.

50% restock  |  30% household  |  20% savings
Example: At the end of the day, your till and cash together total KSh 2,400.
KSh 1,200 stays in the kitchen for tomorrow's stock (flour, fish, charcoal, sukuma, oil).
KSh 720 goes to the household (rent, school, soap, the small things).
KSh 480 goes into a separate savings envelope. Do not touch this for 3 months. After 3 months you will have enough to buy a second jiko (stove) or repair the window frame.

The savings is not optional. It is the seed of every future improvement. Even KSh 100 per day saved is KSh 3,000 per month and KSh 36,000 per year. That is a new door, a new sign, or three months of rent covered if the rains come.

5

M-Pesa Daily Tally

Every evening, before you sleep, three numbers must match. If they do not, something went wrong and you need to find it tonight, not tomorrow.

M-Pesa till total + cash in hand + credit given out = expected from sales
Example: You served 30 plates. Average plate was KSh 130. So you should have KSh 3,900 total.
M-Pesa till message says: KSh 2,100 received today.
Cash in your tin: KSh 1,500.
Two students owe you KSh 100 each (you let them pay later): KSh 200.
Total: 2,100 + 1,500 + 200 = KSh 3,800.
Expected: 3,900. Difference: KSh 100 short. Find it now: a wrong change given, a plate not recorded, or a till you forgot to check.

A small difference every day becomes a big loss every month. The math does not lie. If you do this for 30 days, you will find the leak.

The One Page Summary

Print this. Tape it inside the kitchen door.

  1. Food cost must stay under 35 percent of the selling price.
  2. Break-even is your floor. Below it, you are losing money.
  3. Reorder before you run out. Running out is losing customers.
  4. Split every shilling: 50 stock, 30 household, 20 savings.
  5. Match the till, the cash, and the credit every single evening.
